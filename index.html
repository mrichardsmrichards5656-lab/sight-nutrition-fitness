Your whole health,
in one place.

Clear, accessible guidance on staying active, eating well, sleeping better and looking after your mind — written for people who are blind or partially sighted, and the people who support them.

👋 Why I built this site

After my daughter was diagnosed with Stargardt disease, I found plenty of guidance about the eyes — but very little on staying well in every other way. Health and fitness have always mattered to me, so I built this: free, practical, whole-health guidance for blind and partially sighted people, and a sister site to Sight Atlas UK. I'm not a doctor — always check with yours.

Move more

🏃 Getting active

Why it's worth it

Being active lifts your mood, helps you sleep, and protects your heart — and sight loss needn't stand in the way. The NHS suggests adults aim for around 150 minutes of moderate activity a week (anything that leaves you a little out of breath), plus strengthening activity on two days. It all counts, and small chunks are fine.

Ways to move

  • At home: chair exercises, marching on the spot, and simple strength moves using tins or bottles are easy to do safely in a familiar space.
  • Walking: a known, clutter-free route — alone, with a sighted guide, or with a cane or guide dog — is great everyday activity.
  • Guided running: a sighted guide runs alongside you on a short tether. Many parkruns welcome guide runners.
  • Swimming: a confident, low-risk environment. Ask the pool about lane ropes and counting lengths.
  • Adaptive sport: clubs across the UK offer goalball, tandem cycling, judo, archery, tennis and more.

Staying safe while you move

  • Start gently and build up — there's no rush.
  • Choose familiar spaces and clear the floor of trip hazards.
  • Tell someone when you head out, or exercise with a buddy.
  • If something hurts beyond normal effort, stop and check with a professional.

Eat well

🥗 Eating well

Balance, not counting

You don't need to track calories. The NHS Eatwell Guide keeps it simple: plenty of vegetables and fruit, some wholegrains, some protein (beans, fish, eggs, meat), a little dairy or alternatives, and not too much sugar, salt or fat.

An accessible kitchen

  • Contrast helps: a dark board for light food and a light board for dark food makes things easier to see.
  • Talking and tactile tools: talking scales, jug-level indicators and talking thermometers take the guesswork out of cooking.
  • A place for everything: organising cupboards and the fridge the same way every time saves time and frustration.
  • Labelling: bump-on stickers or a label reader tell tins and packets apart.
  • Reading labels: a phone app with a screen reader can read out ingredients and nutrition information from packaging.

Simple, nourishing meals

You don't need complicated recipes. One-pot meals, batch cooking, and assembling rather than cooking — wholemeal toast with beans, or yoghurt with fruit and oats — all make balanced eating straightforward.

Rest well

😴 Sleep

Sleeping better

  • Keep regular times for going to bed and getting up.
  • Wind down before bed — gentle routine, calm sounds, less screen time.
  • Go easy on caffeine and big meals late in the day.
  • Keep the bedroom cool, quiet and comfortable.

Sight loss and your body clock

Our body clock is partly set by light. Some people with little or no light perception find their sleep pattern drifts later each day — sometimes called Non-24-Hour Sleep-Wake Disorder. If your sleep feels out of step with the world around you, it's worth talking to your GP, as there are treatments that can help.

Mind & mood

🧠 Stress & mind

Your wellbeing matters

Living with sight loss can bring frustration, worry or low mood at times — that's a very human response, and you don't have to manage it alone. Small, steady habits help: keeping a routine, staying connected with people, getting outside, and being kind to yourself.

Things that can ease stress

  • Slow breathing — a few minutes of longer, gentle out-breaths.
  • Gentle activity and time outdoors.
  • Talking to someone you trust, or a peer support group.
  • Breaking tasks into small steps so they feel manageable.

Getting support

If low mood, anxiety or stress is affecting your daily life, please reach out. Your GP can talk through options, including NHS talking therapies. The RNIB Helpline also offers emotional support and someone who understands.

RNIB Helpline: 0303 123 9999 — Mon–Fri 8am–8pm, Sat 9am–1pm.
